Compliance and enforcement

Compliance and enforcement content covers what happens when things go wrong — or when the CRA needs to verify that things are right. This ranges from routine audits through formal objections and appeals to criminal prosecution for tax evasion. Audits

The CRA conducts audits across all program areas:

  • Individual audits (T1) — reviewing reported income, deductions, and credits
  • Corporate audits (T2) — reviewing corporate returns and financial statements
  • GST/HST audits — verifying input tax credit claims and compliance
  • Trust audits (T3) — including enhanced beneficial ownership reporting
  • Payroll audits — verifying source deductions and taxable benefit calculations
  • SR&ED claim reviews — desk reviews, on-site reviews, and technical assessments
  • Large file case management — dedicated audit teams for large corporations
  • Underground economy and sharing economy audits

Objections and appeals

  • Notice of Objection — filed within 90 days of the date on the notice of assessment or reassessment (Form T400A for individuals, or by letter)
  • CRA Appeals Branch — conducts an independent review of the assessment (separate from Audit)
  • Tax Court of Canada — if the objection is denied or 90 days elapse without a decision
  • Informal procedure — for amounts under $25,000 per issue or losses under $50,000 (simpler, faster)
  • General procedure — for larger amounts (more formal, right to claim costs)
  • Federal Court of Appeal — appeals from Tax Court decisions
  • Supreme Court of Canada — final appeal (leave to appeal required)

Key pamphlet: P148 (Resolving Your Dispute: Objection and Appeal Rights Under the Income Tax Act)

Voluntary Disclosures Program (VDP)

The VDP allows taxpayers to come forward to correct errors or omissions in their tax affairs before the CRA discovers them.

  • Conditions: disclosure must be voluntary, complete, involve a penalty, and cover information more than one year past due
  • General application stream — relief from penalties and potential partial interest relief
  • Limited program — applies to unreported income, unfiled returns, and errors on past returns
  • No relief from tax owing is provided — only penalties and some interest
  • Information circular: IC00-1

Taxpayer relief provisions

  • Cancel or waive penalties and interest — within a 10-year limitation period
  • Accept late-filed elections — when the taxpayer missed a deadline for an election under the ITA
  • Refund or reduce amounts beyond the normal 3-year reassessment period
  • Form RC4288 — Request for Taxpayer Relief
  • Information circular: IC07-1

Collections

When a taxpayer owes money to the CRA, the collections process follows an escalating series of steps:

  1. Notices of assessment showing balance owing
  2. Legal warnings and final notices
  3. Set-off — applying refunds or credits against the debt
  4. Requirement to pay (garnishment) — directing employers, banks, or accounts receivable debtors to pay the CRA
  5. Certification in Federal Court — creates a judgment equivalent to a court order
  6. Liens on real property
  7. Seizure and sale of assets
  8. Third-party assessments (director's liability) — directors can be held personally liable for corporate GST/HST, payroll, and income tax debts

Payment arrangements can be negotiated. Financial hardship provisions exist.

Penalties

  • Late-filing penalty — 5% of balance owing + 1% per month late, up to 12 months; doubled for repeat offenders within a 3-year period
  • Failure to report income — 10% of the unreported amount on the second occurrence within 4 years (federal)
  • Gross negligence penalty — 50% of the understated tax or overstated credits
  • Third-party civil penalties — for advisors and tax preparers who participate in making false statements
  • Mandatory disclosure penalties — for failing to report reportable or notifiable transactions
  • Transfer pricing penalty — 10% of the adjustment when documentation was not maintained
  • Late/failure to file information returns — various penalties depending on the form

Criminal investigations and prosecution

  • The CRA's Criminal Investigations Program investigates suspected tax evasion and fraud
  • Cases are referred to the Public Prosecution Service of Canada
  • Convicted tax evaders are publicly named on the CRA website
  • Penalties can include fines (up to 200% of tax evaded) and imprisonment

Information circular: IC73-10 (Tax Evasion)
