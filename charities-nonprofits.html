Charities and non-profits

The CRA regulates registered charities and monitors non-profit organizations. Understanding the distinction is important — only registered charities can issue official donation receipts, and registration comes with significant ongoing obligations.

Registered charities

A registered charity is a charitable organization, public foundation, or private foundation that has been approved by the CRA's Charities Directorate.

  • Charitable purposes: relief of poverty, advancement of education, advancement of religion, or other purposes beneficial to the community
  • Registration: organizations apply for registered status; once approved, receive an RR program account under their Business Number
  • Donation receipts: only registered charities (and other qualified donees) can issue official donation receipts that allow donors to claim the charitable donations tax credit
  • Three types: charitable organizations (carry on their own activities), public foundations (primarily fund other charities, arm's-length board), private foundations (controlled by a single donor or related group)

Annual filing (T3010)

  • T3010 (Registered Charity Information Return) — must be filed annually within 6 months of fiscal year-end
  • Includes financial information, activities, programs, and compensation details
  • T3010 is publicly available — anyone can look up a charity's return
  • Failure to file for 2 consecutive years leads to revocation of registered status

Disbursement quota

Registered charities with assets over $1 million (not used directly in charitable activities or administration) must disburse at least 3.5% of those assets annually on charitable activities. This rule ensures charities actively use their resources rather than simply accumulating endowments.

Sanctions and compliance

  • Suspensions — the charity cannot issue donation receipts during the suspension period
  • Monetary penalties — intermediate sanctions short of revocation
  • Revocation — the ultimate sanction; the charity loses registered status and must pay a revocation tax on remaining assets
  • Direction and control — charities carrying on activities through intermediaries must maintain direction and control over the use of their resources

Qualified donees

Only qualified donees can issue official donation receipts. The list includes:

  • Registered charities
  • Registered Canadian amateur athletic associations (RCAAAs)
  • Registered journalism organizations (RJOs) — related to the digital news subscription tax credit and Qualified Canadian Journalism Organization (QCJO) status
  • Registered national arts service organizations
  • Registered housing corporations
  • Municipalities in Canada
  • The United Nations and its agencies
  • Certain prescribed foreign universities
  • Her Majesty in right of Canada or a province

Non-profit organizations (NPOs)

NPOs are distinct from registered charities:

  • Exempt from income tax under paragraph 149(1)(l) of the Income Tax Act
  • Cannot issue donation receipts
  • T1044 (Non-Profit Organization Information Return) — required when certain revenue or asset thresholds are met
  • Organized exclusively for social welfare, civic improvement, pleasure, recreation, or any other purpose except profit
