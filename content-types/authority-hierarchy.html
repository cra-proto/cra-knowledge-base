The authority hierarchy
How all CRA content types relate to each other, from legislation to campaigns

The authority hierarchy

CRA content types exist in a hierarchy from most legally authoritative to least. Understanding this hierarchy helps content designers know what sources to reference and how much weight to give different types of CRA content.

The hierarchy

  1. The Income Tax Act / Excise Tax Act — the law itself (not CRA content, but the foundation everything rests on)
  2. Income Tax Regulations — delegated legislation made under the Act
  3. Advance income tax rulings — binding CRA commitments on specific proposed transactions
  4. Income Tax Folios — the CRA's general interpretive positions (replacing ITs)
  5. Interpretation Bulletins — legacy equivalent of folios (being phased out)
  6. Information Circulars — CRA administrative procedures
  7. Technical interpretations — non-binding responses to specific questions
  8. Guides — comprehensive instructions for completing returns and navigating topics
  9. Pamphlets — short plain-language overviews
  10. Forms — the documents that implement everything above
  11. Campaign pages — promotional and awareness content
  12. News releases — announcements of changes
  13. "Read me" pages — navigational entry points

Practical implications for content designers

When to reference what

  • Writing a guide or help page? Reference the relevant folio for the CRA's interpretive position
  • Unsure about a specific scenario? Check technical interpretations for precedent
  • Writing about a CRA process (audits, appeals, etc.)? Reference the relevant information circular
  • Never treat a campaign page or news release as a source of tax law — they are communications tools

When content types disagree

Higher-authority sources prevail. If a guide says one thing but the folio says another, the folio is more authoritative. If a folio seems to conflict with a court decision, the court decision prevails (the CRA may update the folio). The Act itself always wins.

The complete chain — an example

To see how all content types connect, follow the disability tax credit through the hierarchy:

  1. The Income Tax Act — sections 118.3 and 118.4 define the DTC
  2. Regulations — prescribe the form and medical practitioner requirements
  3. Folio S1-F1-C2 — CRA's interpretation of DTC eligibility criteria
  4. Guide RC4064 — Disability-Related Information (explains DTC to taxpayers)
  5. Form T2201 — Disability Tax Credit Certificate (the document taxpayers submit)
  6. Campaign page — promoting DTC awareness ("You may be eligible")
  7. News release — announcing changes to DTC eligibility criteria

Each layer serves a different audience and purpose, but all trace back to the same provisions of the Act.
