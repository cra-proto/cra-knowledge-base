Individual tax

The T1 individual income tax return is the central hub of the CRA ecosystem. Filing a T1 is what connects individuals to benefits, credits, and registered plan contribution room. This page covers every aspect of individual tax that a content designer may encounter.

Filing the T1 return

The T1 General (Income Tax and Benefit Return) is the core annual return. It comes with more than a dozen supporting schedules and can be filed through several channels.

Filing methods
  • NETFILE — electronic filing via certified tax software (most common method)
  • EFILE — electronic filing through a tax professional or preparer
  • File My Return — automated phone filing for eligible simple returns
  • Paper filing — mailing a completed return to a tax centre
  • Auto-fill my return — pre-populating returns with data from My Account
  • Express NOA — immediate Notice of Assessment after NETFILE
  • ReFILE — electronic method to change a previously filed return
Key schedules
  • Schedule 1 — Federal Tax calculation
  • Schedule 2 — Federal Amounts Transferred from Spouse or Common-Law Partner
  • Schedule 3 — Capital Gains or Losses
  • Schedule 4 — Statement of Investment Income
  • Schedule 5 — Amounts for Spouse or Common-Law Partner and Dependants
  • Schedule 6 — Canada Workers Benefit
  • Schedule 7 — RRSP, PRPP and SPP Unused Contributions, Transfers, and HBP or LLP Activities
  • Schedule 8 — CPP Contributions and Overpayment
  • Schedule 9 — Donations and Gifts
  • Schedule 11 — Federal Tuition, Education, and Textbook Amounts
  • Schedule 14 — Climate Action Incentive Payment
  • Provincial and territorial schedules (ON428, BC428, AB428, etc.)
My Account for individuals

My Account is the CRA's self-serve portal for individuals. Through it, taxpayers can:

  • View notices of assessment and reassessment
  • View or change return information, address, direct deposit, and marital status
  • View benefit and credit payment statements
  • Check RRSP and TFSA contribution limits and carryforward amounts
  • Manage authorized representatives
  • Submit documents and register for online mail

Non-refundable tax credits

Non-refundable credits reduce tax payable but cannot create a refund on their own. They are calculated at 15% of the eligible amount on the federal return.

Personal amount credits
  • Basic personal amount — available to all taxpayers
  • Age amount — for taxpayers 65 and older, income-tested
  • Spouse or common-law partner amount — based on spouse's net income
  • Eligible dependant amount — for single parents or those supporting a dependant
  • Canada caregiver credit — for dependants with a physical or mental infirmity
  • Pension income amount — up to $2,000 of qualifying pension income
Disability, medical, and education
  • Disability tax credit (DTC) — requires approved Form T2201; transferable to supporting person
  • Medical expenses — lines 33099 and 33199, threshold-based
  • Tuition tax credit — Schedule 11, for eligible tuition fees
  • Interest on student loans — federal and provincial student loan interest
Housing, charitable, and other credits
  • First-time home buyers' tax credit (HBTC) — $10,000 credit = $1,500 tax reduction
  • Home accessibility tax credit (HATC) — up to $20,000 in eligible expenses for seniors or DTC-eligible individuals
  • Charitable donations — Schedule 9; first $200 at 15%, remainder at 29% or 33%
  • Canada employment credit — for employment income earners
  • Digital news subscription tax credit
  • Volunteer firefighters' and search and rescue volunteers' tax credit
  • Teacher and early childhood educator school supply tax credit
  • Political contribution tax credit — federal political donations
  • Foreign tax credit — Form T2209, to avoid double taxation

Refundable tax credits

Refundable credits can generate a refund even if no tax is owed. They are the primary mechanism through which the CRA delivers benefits.

  • GST/HST credit — quarterly payments for low and modest income individuals and families
  • Canada Workers Benefit (CWB) — for low-income workers, plus disability supplement
  • Climate Action Incentive Payment (CAIP) — quarterly payments for residents of fuel-charge provinces
  • Canada Child Benefit (CCB) — monthly income-tested payment for families with children under 18
  • Canada training credit — $250 per year accumulation for eligible training expenses
  • Multigenerational home renovation tax credit — up to $7,500 refundable (15% of $50,000 in qualifying expenses)
  • Return of fuel charge proceeds to farmers tax credit
  • Teacher and early childhood educator school supply tax credit (refundable portion)

Key deductions

Deductions reduce taxable income before tax is calculated. Unlike credits, their value depends on the taxpayer's marginal tax rate.

  • RRSP deduction — Schedule 7; 18% of prior year earned income, subject to dollar maximum minus pension adjustment
  • FHSA deduction — First Home Savings Account contributions; $8,000 annual limit
  • Union and professional dues
  • Child care expenses — Form T778
  • Disability supports deduction
  • Moving expenses — Form T1-M; must move 40+ km closer to work or school
  • Support payments made — deductible spousal support
  • Carrying charges and interest expenses — investment-related borrowing costs
  • Employment expenses — Form T777 or T777S (simplified for home office)
  • Northern residents deductions — Form T2222; residency deduction and travel
  • Capital gains deduction — for qualified small business shares, farm or fishing property
  • Clergy residence deduction — Form T1223

Types of income

Different income types are reported on different lines and slips. Understanding the source determines the correct reporting treatment.

  • Employment income — T4 slip
  • Self-employment income — Form T2125 (business, professional, commission), T2042 (farming), T2121 (fishing)
  • Pension income — T4A, T4A(P), T4A(OAS), T4RIF, T4RSP slips
  • Investment income — interest, dividends, capital gains; T3, T5, T5008 slips
  • Rental income — Form T776
  • Employment insurance benefits — T4E slip
  • Social assistance and workers' compensation
  • Foreign income — must be reported in Canadian dollars
  • Sharing economy income — ride-sharing, short-term rentals
  • Cryptocurrency and digital assets — treated as commodity; barter rules apply
  • Tips and gratuities
  • Scholarships and research grants

Life events and situations

Major life changes trigger specific tax obligations or opportunities. Content designers frequently write about these scenarios.

  • Getting married or becoming common-law
  • Separation or divorce
  • Having a child
  • Death of a taxpayer (final return, rights or things return, optional returns)
  • Bankruptcy
  • Leaving Canada (emigration — deemed disposition)
  • Entering Canada (immigration — fresh-start cost base)
  • Students
  • Newcomers to Canada
  • Indigenous peoples
  • Persons with disabilities
  • Seniors
  • Northern residents
  • Members of the Canadian Armed Forces
  • Employees working from home
