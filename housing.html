Housing

Housing-related tax measures span credits, deductions, rebates, and compliance rules. They touch individual tax (credits and deductions), GST/HST (rebates), and even a standalone annual tax (the UHT). A content designer working on housing content needs to understand how these measures interact.

Underused Housing Tax (UHT)

An annual 1% tax on the value of vacant or underused residential property in Canada. Primarily targets non-Canadian owners, but some Canadian owners must also file even if exempt.

  • Form: UHT-2900 (Underused Housing Tax Return and Election Form)
  • Who must file: all owners who are not excluded owners (excluded = Canadian citizens or permanent residents who are individuals, not trustees, and not partners)
  • Exemptions from tax (but not from filing): primary place of residence, qualifying occupancy (relative, arm's-length tenant for 180+ days), new construction, uninhabitable, disaster-affected
  • Penalty for late filing: minimum $5,000 for individuals, $10,000 for corporations

Home buyers' measures

  • First Home Savings Account (FHSA) — $8,000 annual / $40,000 lifetime; deductible contributions with tax-free qualifying withdrawals (see Savings and registered plans)
  • Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) — withdraw up to $60,000 from RRSP for a first home; repay over 15 years (see RRSP section)
  • First-time home buyers' tax credit (HBTC) — $10,000 non-refundable credit = $1,500 federal tax reduction

Accessibility and renovation credits

  • Home accessibility tax credit (HATC) — up to $20,000 in eligible expenses for seniors (65+) or DTC-eligible individuals; non-refundable at 15% = up to $3,000
  • Multigenerational home renovation tax credit — up to $50,000 in eligible renovation expenses to create a secondary suite for a senior or DTC-eligible person; refundable at 15% = up to $7,500

GST/HST housing rebates

  • GST/HST New Housing Rebate — rebate on portion of GST/HST paid on a new home purchase or substantial renovation; Form GST190
  • GST/HST New Residential Rental Property Rebate — for builders of new rental housing; Form GST524

Residential property flipping rule

Effective 2023: profits from the disposition of residential property held for less than 365 days are deemed business income (not capital gains), unless a life event exception applies (death, disability, separation, job relocation, safety threat, insolvency).

This means the full profit is taxable with no capital gains inclusion rate and no principal residence exemption.

Principal residence exemption

The capital gain on the disposition of a principal residence is exempt from tax. Key rules:

  • Form T2091 must be completed when designating a property as principal residence upon sale
  • Only one property per family unit can be designated as principal residence per year
  • The 1+ rule: the formula gives an extra year, so a property held for n years of designation exempts (n+1)/total years of ownership of the gain
  • Must be ordinarily inhabited by the taxpayer, their spouse, or child during the year

Rental income and short-term rentals

  • Rental income — reported on Form T776 (Statement of Real Estate Rentals)
  • Short-term rental restrictions (effective 2024+) — denial of rental expense deductions for non-compliant short-term rentals in jurisdictions that require licensing or registration
  • Guide T4036 — Rental Income Guide

Enhanced capital gains inclusion rate

Effective June 25, 2024: the capital gains inclusion rate increased to 66.67% (two-thirds) on capital gains above $250,000 for individuals. The first $250,000 in net capital gains per year remains at the 50% inclusion rate. For corporations and trusts, the 66.67% rate applies to all capital gains.
