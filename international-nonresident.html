International and non-resident

International tax is among the most complex areas of CRA content. Non-residents may be subject to Part I tax (on Canadian-source business or employment income), Part XIII tax (withholding on passive income), or both. Tax treaties modify these rules. Content designers working in this area should understand the basic framework.

Non-resident individuals

  • Part I tax — applies when carrying on business in Canada, employed in Canada, or disposing of taxable Canadian property
  • Part XIII tax — 25% withholding (or treaty-reduced rate) on Canadian-source passive income: dividends, interest, rents, royalties, pensions, retiring allowances
  • Section 216 election — allows filing a T1 return on net rental income rather than paying 25% on gross; NR6 filed in advance to reduce withholding to net amount
  • Section 217 election — allows filing on certain pension and benefit income if beneficial
  • NR4 reporting — payers report amounts paid to non-residents on NR4 slips
  • NR5 — Application for a Reduction in Non-Resident Tax Withholding
  • NR301/NR302/NR303 — Declaration of Eligibility for Benefits under a Tax Treaty

Related repos: part-xiii-non-res, s216, direct-deposit

Non-resident corporations

  • Branch tax (Part XIV) — 25% on after-tax income not invested in qualifying property in Canada (reduced by treaty)
  • Treaty-reduced withholding rates — most treaties reduce dividend withholding to 5%–15%, interest to 0%–10%, royalties to 0%–10%

Taxable Canadian property

When non-residents dispose of taxable Canadian property, they need a certificate of compliance before the purchaser can release proceeds.

  • Form T2062 — Request by a Non-Resident for a Certificate of Compliance
  • Form T2062A — for Canadian resource property or timber resource property
  • Taxable Canadian property includes: real property in Canada, business assets in Canada, shares of certain corporations, partnership interests
  • Purchasers must withhold 25% if the certificate is not obtained before closing

Tax treaties

Canada has more than 90 bilateral tax treaties. They serve to:

  • Reduce or eliminate double taxation
  • Reduce withholding tax rates on cross-border payments
  • Determine residence for treaty purposes (tie-breaker rules when a person is resident of both countries)
  • Provide for Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) for dispute resolution
  • Enable exchange of information between tax authorities
  • Provide competent authority proceedings for transfer pricing and other disputes

Transfer pricing

  • Arm's length principle — Section 247 of the ITA requires that transactions between related parties reflect amounts that would be agreed by arm's length parties
  • Documentation requirements — contemporaneous documentation must be maintained
  • Advance Pricing Arrangements (APAs) — unilateral, bilateral, or multilateral agreements with tax authorities on pricing methodology
  • Transfer pricing penalty — 10% of the adjustment amount if documentation was not maintained
  • Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) — large multinationals must file CbCR reports
  • Form T106 — Information Return of Non-Arm's Length Transactions with Non-Residents

Foreign reporting obligations

  • T1135 — Foreign Income Verification Statement; required when specified foreign property exceeds $100,000 at any time during the year (simplified and detailed reporting methods)
  • T1134 — Information Return for Foreign Affiliates
  • T1141 — Information Return for Contributions to Non-Resident Trusts
  • T1142 — Information Return for Distributions from and Indebtedness to a Non-Resident Trust
  • Mandatory Disclosure Rules — reportable transactions and notifiable transactions must be disclosed

Emigrants and immigrants

Emigration (leaving Canada)

  • Deemed disposition at fair market value on all property (departure tax)
  • Form T1161 — List of Properties by an Emigrant of Canada
  • Form T1243 — Deemed Disposition of Property by an Emigrant of Canada
  • Form T1244 — Election to Defer Payment of Tax on departure
  • Exceptions: real property in Canada, RRSP/RRIF/pension, stock options, certain other Canadian property

Immigration (entering Canada)

  • Fresh start rules — cost base of property is reset to FMV at date of entry
  • File T1 for the portion of the year as a Canadian resident
  • TFSA contribution room begins accumulating from the year the newcomer turns 18 and is a resident
  • RRSP room begins accumulating from earned income reported on Canadian T1 returns
  • Guide T4055 — Newcomers to Canada
