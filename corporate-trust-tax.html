Corporate and trust tax

Corporate and trust tax involve the most complex areas of the Income Tax Act. Content designers working on T2 or T3 content should understand the key concepts that drive how these returns work and how corporate tax integrates with personal tax through the dividend mechanism.

T2 Corporation Income Tax Return

The T2 return is filed annually by every corporation resident in Canada (and non-resident corporations carrying on business in Canada). It comes with an extensive set of schedules.

Key schedules
  • Schedule 1 — Net Income for Income Tax Purposes
  • Schedule 3 — Dividends Received, Taxable Dividends Paid, and Part IV Tax
  • Schedule 4 — Corporation Loss Continuity and Application
  • Schedule 5 — Tax Calculation Supplementary
  • Schedule 7 — Aggregate Investment Income and Active Business Income
  • Schedule 8 — Capital Cost Allowance (CCA)
  • Schedule 9 — Related and Associated Corporations
  • Schedule 31 — Investment Tax Credit (SR&ED and other ITCs)
  • Schedule 50 — Shareholder Information
  • Schedule 53/54 — GRIP and LRIP (General Rate / Low Rate Income Pool)
  • Schedule 100–141 — GIFI (General Index of Financial Information — financial statements)
  • Schedule 200 — T2 Bar Code Return
  • Schedule 300+ — Provincial tax schedules
Filing requirements
  • Most corporations must file electronically (Corporation Internet Filing)
  • Filing deadline: 6 months after fiscal year-end
  • Tax payment deadline: 2 months after fiscal year-end (3 months for CCPCs claiming the small business deduction with taxable income under the business limit in the prior year)
  • T2 Short Return available for eligible small or inactive corporations

Key corporate tax concepts

Small business deduction (SBD)

Reduced tax rate on the first $500,000 of active business income for Canadian-controlled private corporations (CCPCs). The business limit is shared among associated corporations (Schedule 23). The SBD is reduced when taxable capital employed in Canada exceeds $10 million (grind begins) and eliminated at $15 million.

Integration and dividend mechanism

The Canadian tax system is designed so that income earned through a corporation and paid out as dividends should bear roughly the same total tax as income earned directly by an individual. This is achieved through:

  • Eligible dividends (from income taxed at the general corporate rate) — grossed up by 38%, with a dividend tax credit of 6/11 of the gross-up
  • Non-eligible dividends (from income taxed at the small business rate) — grossed up by 15%, with a dividend tax credit of 9/13 of the gross-up
  • GRIP/LRIP pools track how much income was taxed at which rate, determining which type of dividend can be designated
  • RDTOH (Refundable Dividend Tax on Hand) — refundable tax paid by the corporation is refunded when dividends are paid ($1 refund for every $3 in dividends)
  • Capital dividend account (CDA) — the tax-free portion of capital gains can be paid as tax-free capital dividends
Salary vs. dividend decision

A key planning consideration for owner-managers: salary creates RRSP room and CPP contributions; dividends do not. Each has different integration outcomes depending on the province and the individual's other income.

Other corporate programs
  • Accelerated Investment Incentive — enhanced CCA in first year of acquisition
  • Immediate expensing for CCPCs — up to $1.5 million of eligible property
  • Manufacturing and processing profits deduction
  • Part IV tax — tax on portfolio dividends received by private corporations
  • Canada Carbon Rebate for Small Businesses — Schedule 63
  • Canada Recovery Dividend Tax — Schedule 73 (one-time tax on large financial institutions)

T3 Trust Income Tax and Information Return

Trust types
  • Inter vivos trusts — created during the settlor's lifetime
  • Testamentary trusts — created by a will or court order upon death
  • Graduated Rate Estates (GRE) — available for the first 36 months after death; gets graduated tax rates instead of top marginal rate
  • Qualified Disability Trusts (QDT) — beneficiary must be DTC-eligible; also gets graduated rates
  • Bare trusts — new reporting requirements introduced in 2023 (trustee holds legal title but has no discretion)
  • Mutual fund trusts, REITs, SIFTs — specialized rules for investment vehicles
Trust filing and reporting
  • T3 Summary and T3 slips — allocations to beneficiaries (income retains its character when flowed through)
  • Trust reporting — enhanced beneficial ownership requirements since 2023
  • The T3010 (Registered Charity Information Return) is a separate return from the T3 trust return
