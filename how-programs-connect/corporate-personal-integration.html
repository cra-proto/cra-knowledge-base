Corporate and personal integration

The Canadian tax system is designed so that income earned through a corporation and distributed as dividends should bear roughly the same total tax as income earned directly. This is called "integration" and it is fundamental to understanding corporate tax content.

How integration works

  1. Corporation earns business income
  2. Corporation pays corporate tax (Part I) at the applicable rate
  3. After-tax income is available for dividends
  4. Dividends are paid to shareholders
  5. Shareholders report dividends on their T1, with a gross-up and dividend tax credit to offset the corporate tax already paid

Two types of dividends

TypeSourceGross-upFederal DTC
Eligible dividendsIncome taxed at the general corporate rate38%6/11 of gross-up
Non-eligible dividendsIncome taxed at the small business rate15%9/13 of gross-up

Tracking pools

  • GRIP (General Rate Income Pool) — tracks income that has been taxed at the general rate and can therefore support eligible dividend designations
  • LRIP (Low Rate Income Pool) — tracks income taxed at the small business rate; non-eligible dividends must be paid from LRIP before eligible dividends
  • RDTOH (Refundable Dividend Tax on Hand) — a refundable tax paid by the corporation that is refunded when dividends are paid ($1 refund for every $3 in dividends)
  • Capital dividend account (CDA) — the tax-free half of capital gains can be distributed as tax-free capital dividends

Salary vs. dividends

A key consideration for owner-managers (and for content written for small business owners):

  • Salary creates RRSP contribution room, CPP contributions, and is deductible to the corporation
  • Dividends do not create RRSP room or CPP contributions, but can be more tax-efficient depending on province and income level
