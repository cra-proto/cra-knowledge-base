Filing to benefit delivery

This is the chain that runs every time an individual files a T1 return. Understanding it helps content designers explain why filing is essential and how benefits are calculated.

The chain

  1. Individual files T1 return — reporting all income sources from information slips and other records
  2. CRA assesses the return — issues a Notice of Assessment (NOA)
  3. Net income and adjusted family net income are calculated — these are the key inputs for benefit formulas
  4. Figures flow automatically to benefit calculation systems
  5. CCB is recalculated each July based on the prior year's return (July 2026 payments are based on the 2025 T1)
  6. GST/HST credit is recalculated each July
  7. CAIP is recalculated each April
  8. CWB is calculated directly on the T1 itself as a refundable credit
  9. Provincial and territorial benefits are calculated through T1 provincial schedules
  10. If a return is not filed, benefits are suspended or stopped

Timing implications

Because benefits are recalculated based on the prior year's return, there is always a lag. This means:

  • A significant income change in 2025 will not affect CCB payments until July 2026
  • A taxpayer who loses their job will continue receiving benefits based on their higher prior-year income until the next recalculation
  • Filing early (vs. at the deadline) does not accelerate benefit recalculation — it always happens on the program's schedule

The marital status connection

Adjusted family net income includes the spouse's net income. When marital status changes (marriage, common-law, separation), benefits are recalculated based on the new family net income. Taxpayers must notify the CRA of marital status changes — failure to do so can result in overpayments that must be repaid.
