The T1 return as master key

The T1 individual income tax return is the central node of the CRA ecosystem. Almost everything flows through it.

What the T1 unlocks

  • Benefits: filing a T1 is required to receive CCB, GST/HST credit, CWB, CAIP, GIS (for seniors), and all provincial and territorial benefits administered by the CRA
  • Net income (line 23600) and adjusted family net income — the two figures that determine benefit amounts for CCB, GST/HST credit, CWB, CAIP, CESG and CLB (for RESP), CDSG and CDSB (for RDSP), and GIS
  • Taxable income (line 26000) — determines federal and provincial tax payable
  • RRSP deduction limit — computed from earned income reported on the T1, minus pension adjustments from employer T4 slips
  • TFSA contribution room — accumulates annually for residents 18 and older (CRA tracks through T1 filing data)
  • CWB advance payments — based on the prior year's T1

The consequence of not filing

This is the single most important point for content designers: if a T1 is not filed, benefits stop. It does not matter if the individual is eligible. The system requires the return to calculate and deliver benefits. This is why CRA campaigns and CVITP outreach constantly encourage filing — especially among populations with exempt income (like status Indians under Section 87) who might assume they do not need to file.

What flows into the T1

The T1 is itself fed by dozens of information slips:

  • T4 (employment income)
  • T4A (pension, other income)
  • T4E (EI benefits)
  • T3 (trust income allocations)
  • T5 (investment income)
  • T5008 (securities transactions)
  • RRSP contribution receipts
  • T2202 (tuition amounts from institutions)
  • Charitable donation receipts

These slips are also reported to the CRA directly by employers, financial institutions, and educational institutions — creating a cross-referencing system that underlies audit selection.
