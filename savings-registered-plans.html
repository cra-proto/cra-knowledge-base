Savings and registered plans

Registered savings plans are tax-advantaged accounts governed by the Income Tax Act. Each has distinct contribution rules, tax treatment, and reporting requirements. Understanding how they interact is critical — contribution room for RRSPs depends on T1 earned income and pension adjustments; RDSP eligibility depends on DTC approval; FHSA balances can transfer to RRSPs.

Tax-Free Savings Account (TFSA)

  • Contribution room: $7,000 per year (as of 2024, indexed); cumulative since 2009 for residents 18 and older
  • Tax treatment: contributions are not deductible, but investment income and withdrawals are completely tax-free
  • Withdrawals: re-added to contribution room the following calendar year
  • Over-contribution penalty: 1% per month on excess amounts
  • Non-residents: no new contribution room accumulates; contributions while non-resident attract 1% per month tax
  • Death: FMV at date of death tax-free to beneficiaries; income earned after death is taxable unless successor holder is spouse

Related repos: TFSA-cop, TFSA-test, TFSA-cop-test2

Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP)

  • Contribution limit: 18% of prior year earned income, subject to annual dollar maximum, minus pension adjustment (PA)
  • Deduction: contributions reduce taxable income in the year claimed (or carried forward)
  • Home Buyers' Plan (HBP): withdraw up to $60,000 for a first home; repay over 15 years or face income inclusion
  • Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP): withdraw up to $10,000 per year ($20,000 total) for full-time education; repay over 10 years
  • Spousal RRSP: contributor claims the deduction, but the plan belongs to the spouse (attribution rules apply for 3 calendar years)
  • Maturity: must convert to RRIF, annuity, or lump sum by December 31 of the year the planholder turns 71
  • Over-contribution buffer: $2,000 lifetime over-contribution is penalty-free; amounts beyond that attract 1% per month

Registered Retirement Income Fund (RRIF)

  • Minimum withdrawal: prescribed percentages that increase with age (no minimum in year of conversion)
  • No maximum withdrawal
  • Tax withheld on amounts above the annual minimum
  • Conversion from RRSP: mandatory by December 31 of the year the planholder turns 71
  • Pension income splitting: RRIF income qualifies for up to 50% allocation to spouse if the annuitant is 65 or older (Form T1032)
  • Pension income amount: RRIF withdrawals qualify for the $2,000 non-refundable pension income credit if 65+

Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP)

  • Lifetime contribution limit: $50,000 per beneficiary (no annual limit, but grants are capped annually)
  • Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG): 20% match on first $2,500 contributed per year = up to $500 per year, $7,200 lifetime
  • Additional CESG: extra 10%–20% on first $500 for lower-income families
  • Canada Learning Bond (CLB): up to $2,000 for children in lower-income families (no contribution required)
  • Provincial incentives: SAGES (Saskatchewan), ACES (Alberta), QESI (Quebec)
  • Education Assistance Payments (EAPs): grants and income paid to the student (taxable to student)
  • Accumulated Income Payments (AIPs): income withdrawn by subscriber if beneficiary does not pursue education (subject to tax + 20% penalty, or transfer to RRSP)

Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP)

  • Eligibility: beneficiary must be approved for the disability tax credit (DTC — Form T2201)
  • Lifetime contribution limit: $200,000 (no annual limit)
  • Canada Disability Savings Grant (CDSG): up to $3,500 per year, $70,000 lifetime — matching rate depends on family income and contribution amount
  • Canada Disability Savings Bond (CDSB): up to $1,000 per year, $20,000 lifetime — no contribution required, for lower-income beneficiaries
  • Assistance holdback amount: 10-year rule — government grants and bonds received in the preceding 10 years must be repaid if withdrawn early
  • DTC loss: losing DTC eligibility triggers RDSP wind-down rules

First Home Savings Account (FHSA)

  • Annual contribution limit: $8,000 (lifetime $40,000)
  • Tax treatment (hybrid): contributions are tax-deductible like RRSP; withdrawals for a qualifying home purchase are tax-free like TFSA
  • Eligibility: must be a first-time home buyer (not owned a home in the current year or any of the 4 preceding calendar years)
  • Account lifetime: 15 years or end of the year the holder turns 71 (whichever is earlier)
  • Transfers: can transfer from RRSP to FHSA (uses FHSA room, not RRSP deduction); can transfer unused FHSA to RRSP/RRIF (no deduction, but no income inclusion)
  • Introduced: 2023

Other registered plans

  • Pooled Registered Pension Plans (PRPPs) — for employees and self-employed; administered by licensed administrators; portable between employers
  • Specified Pension Plans (SPPs) — Saskatchewan Pension Plan is the primary example; treated similarly to RRSP for deduction purposes
  • Deferred Profit Sharing Plans (DPSPs) — employer-only contributions with vesting requirements; connected to pension adjustments
  • Registered Pension Plans (RPPs) — defined benefit (DB) and defined contribution (DC) plans; affect RRSP room through pension adjustments (PA), past service pension adjustments (PSPA), and pension adjustment reversals (PAR)

How plans interconnect

Registered plans do not exist in isolation. Key connections:

  • RRSP room = 18% of earned income − PA ± PSPA/PAR, subject to dollar maximum
  • HBP and LLP withdraw from RRSP and require repayment or income inclusion
  • RRSP → RRIF conversion mandatory by end of year turning 71
  • FHSA ↔ RRSP transfers are possible in both directions with specific rules
  • DTC approval is the gateway to RDSP, which in turn is the gateway to CDSG and CDSB
  • Pension income splitting (Form T1032) applies to RRIF income (if 65+) and RPP income
  • RESP → RRSP: unused RESP income (AIPs) can transfer to the subscriber's RRSP if room is available, avoiding the 20% penalty
